Test whether the motion specified by $\vec{q} = \dfrac{k^2(x \hat{j} - y \hat{i})}{x^2 + y^2} \ (k= \text{constant})$ is a possible motion for an incompressible fluid.
If so, determine the equations of the streamlines.
Also test whether the motion is of the potential kind and if so determine the velocity potential.

$$\vec{q} = \frac{k^2(x \hat{j} - y \hat{i})}{x^2 + y^2} = \frac{-k^2 y}{x^2+y^2} \hat{i} + \frac{k^2 x}{x^2 +y^2} \hat{j} \ (k= \text{constant})$$

$$\begin{align*} \nabla \cdot \vec{q} &= \partialwrt{x} \frac{-k^2 y}{x^2+y^2} + \partialwrt{y} \frac{k^2 x}{x^2 +y^2} \\ &= \frac{k^2 y}{(x^2+y^2)^2}2x - \frac{k^2x}{(x^2+y^2)^2}2y \\ &= 0 \end{align*}$$ Therefore the motion is possible.

The equations of the streamlines are given by

\frac{\d dx}{\d u} &=& \dfrac{dy}{v} &=& \dfrac{dz}{w} \\ \frac{\d dx}{\displaystyle \dfrac{-k^2 y}{x^2+y^2}} &=& \dfrac{dy}{\dfrac{k^2 x}{x^2+y^2}} &=& \dfrac{dz}{0} \\ \dfrac{dx}{-y} &=& \dfrac{dy}{x} &=& \dfrac{dz}{0} \\

$$\begin{align*} \frac{dx}{-y} &= \frac{dy}{x} \\ x dx &= -y dy \\ \frac{x^2}{2} &= - \frac{y^2}{2} + \text{constant}\\ \frac{x^2}{2} + \frac{y^2}{2} &= \text{constant}\\ x^2 + y^2 &= \text{constant} \end{align*}$$

$$\begin{align*} \frac{dx}{-y} &= \frac{dz}{0} \\ dz &= 0 \\ z &= \text{constant} \end{align*}$$

The equations of the streamlines are $$\begin{align*} z &= \text{constant}\\ x^2 + y^2 &= \text{constant} \end{align*}$$

We obtain

$$\begin{align*} \curl \vec{q}&= \crossProduct{ \partialwrt{x} & \partialwrt{y} & \partialwrt{z} \\ \dfrac{-k^2 y}{x^2+y^2} & \dfrac{k^2 x}{x^2+y^2} & 0 }\\ \\ &= \bracket{0-\partialwrt{z}\frac{k^2 x}{x^2+y^2}} \hat{i} - \bracket{0-\partialwrt{z}\frac{-k^2 y}{x^2+y^2}} \hat{j} + \bracket{\partialwrt{x}\frac{k^2 x}{x^2+y^2} + \partialwrt{y}\frac{k^2 y}{x^2+y^2}} \hat{k}\\ &= 0 \hat{i} + 0 \hat{j} + \bracket{ \frac{(x^2+y^2)k^2 - k^2x(2x)}{(x^2+y^2)^2} + \frac{(x^2+y^2)k^2 - k^2y(2y)}{(x^2+y^2)^2} }\hat{k}\\ &= 0 \hat{i} + 0 \hat{j} + \frac{2(x^2+y^2)k^2 - 2k^2(x^2+y^2)}{(x^2+y^2)^2} \hat{k}\\ &= 0 \hat{i} + 0 \hat{j} + 0 \hat{k}\\ &= \vec{0} \end{align*}$$

Therefore the flow is of the potential kind.

Let $\varphi = \varphi(x, y, z)$ be the appropriate velocity potential.

$$-\partialwrt[\varphi]{x} = u = \frac{-k^2 y}{x^2+y^2}, -\partialwrt[\varphi]{y} = v = \frac{k^2 x}{x^2+y^2}, \ -\partialwrt[\varphi]{z} = w = 0$$

Since $\dpartialwrt[\varphi]{z} = 0$, we get $\varphi = \varphi(x, y)$

$$\begin{align*} -\partialwrt[\varphi]{x} &= \frac{-k^2 y}{x^2+y^2} \\ \partialwrt[\varphi]{x} &= \frac{k^2 y}{x^2 +y2} \\ \text{ Let } \ x &= y \tan \theta \\ dx &= y \sec^2 \theta d\theta \\ x^2 + y^2 &= y^2 \tan^2 \theta + y^2 \\ &= y^2 (\tan ^2 \theta + 1)\\ &= y^2 \sec^2 \theta\\ \int \frac{y}{x^2+y^2} dx &= \int \frac{y}{y^2 \sec^2 \theta} y \sec^2 \theta d\theta \\ &= \int d \theta \\ &= \theta \\ &= \tan^{-1} \bracket{\frac{x}{y}}\\ \partialwrt[\varphi]{x} &= \frac{k^2 y}{x^2 +y2} \\ \varphi &= k^2 tan^{-1} \bracket{\frac{x}{y}} + f(y) \tag{1} \\ \text{Let } \tan^{-1}\bracket{\frac{x}{y}} &= \delta \\ \tan \delta &= \frac{x}{y} \\ \sec^2 \delta \partialwrt[\delta]{y} &= -\frac{x}{y^2} \\ \partialwrt[\delta]{y} &= - \cos^2 \delta \frac{x}{y^2} \\ &= -\bracket{\frac{y}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}}^2 \frac{x}{y^2} \\ &= \frac{-x}{x^2 + y^2} \\ \mbox{Eq (1) is } \varphi &= k^2 tan^{-1} \bracket{\frac{x}{y}} + f(y)\\ \partialwrt[\varphi]{y} &= k^2 \bracket{\frac{-x}{x^2 +y^2}} + f'(y) \\ \frac{-k^2x}{x^2 +y^2} &= \frac{-k^2x}{x^2 +y^2} + f'(y) \\ f'(y) &= 0 \\ f(y) &= \text{constant} \end{align*}$$

Therefore Eq(1) gives $\varphi = k^2 tan^{-1} \bracket{\dfrac{x}{y}}$
