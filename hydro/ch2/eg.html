At the point in an incompressible fluid having spherical polar coordinates $(r, \theta, \psi)$, the velocity components are $[ 2Mr^{-3}\cos \theta, Mr^{-3} \sin \theta, 0 ]$, where $M$ is a constant. Show that the velocity is of the potential kind. Find the velocity potential and the equations of the streamlines.

\text{Taking } ds= & dr \hat{r} &+& r d\theta \hat{\theta} &+& r \sin \theta \hat{\psi}\\ \vec{q}= & 2Mr^{-3}\cos \theta \hat{r} &+& Mr^{-2}\sin \theta \hat{\theta} &+& 0 \hat{\psi}\\

We obtain

$$\begin{align*} \curl \vec{q}&= \frac{1}{r^2 \sin \theta} \begin{vmatrix} \hat{r} & \hat{\theta} & \hat{\psi} \\ \partialwrt{r} & \partialwrt{\theta} & \partialwrt{\psi} \\ 2Mr^{-3}\cos \theta & Mr^{-3}\sin \theta & 0 \end{vmatrix}\\ &= \frac{1}{r^2 \sin \theta} \bracket{ \bracket{0-\partialwrt{\psi}(Mr^{-3}\sin \theta)} \hat{r} - \bracket{0-\partialwrt{\psi}(2Mr^{-3}\cos \theta)} \hat{\theta} + \bracket{\partialwrt{r}(Mr^{-2}\sin \theta) - \partialwrt{\theta}(2Mr^{-3}\cos \theta)\hat{\psi}} }\\ &= \frac{1}{r^2 \sin \theta} \bracket{ 0 \hat{r} + 0 \hat{\theta} + \bracket(-2Mr^{-3}\sin \theta - 2Mr^{-3}\cos \theta)\hat{\psi} }\\ &= 0 \hat{r} + 0 \hat{\theta} + 0 \hat{\psi}\\ &= \vec{0} \end{align*}$$

Therefore the flow is of the potential kind.

Let $\varphi = \varphi(r, \theta, \psi)$ be the appropriate velocity potential.

$$-\partialwrt[\varphi]{r} = 2Mr^{-3}\cos \theta,\ - \frac{\partial \varphi}{r \partial \theta} = Mr^{-3}\sin \theta,\ \frac{\partial \varphi}{r \sin \theta \partial \psi} = 0$$

Since $\partialwrt[\varphi]{\psi} = 0$, we get $\varphi = \varphi(r, \theta)$

$$\begin{align*} -\partialwrt[\varphi]{r} &= 2Mr^{-3}\cos \theta \\ \partial \varphi &= -2Mr^{-3}\cos \theta \partial r \\ %\text{Integrating both sides with respect to } r, \\ \varphi &= \frac{-2Mr^{-2}\cos \theta}{-2} + f(\theta) \\ \varphi &= Mr^{-2}\cos \theta + f(\theta) \tag{1} \\ \partialwrt[\varphi]{\theta} &= - Mr^{-2}\sin \theta + f'(\theta) \\ -Mr^{-2}\sin \theta &= - Mr^{-2}\sin \theta + f'(\theta) \\ f'(\theta) &= 0 \\ f(\theta) &= \text{ constant } \\ \end{align*}$$

Therefore Eq(1) gives $\varphi = Mr^{-2} \cos \theta$

The equations of the stramlines are given by

\frac{dr}{2Mr^{-3}\cos \theta} &=& \frac{rd\theta}{Mr^{-3}\sin \theta} &=& \frac{r \sin \theta d \psi}{0} \\ \frac{dr}{2 \cos \theta} &=& \frac{rd\theta}{\sin \theta} &=& \frac{r \sin \theta d \psi}{0} \\

$$\begin{align*} \frac{dr}{2 \cos \theta} &= \frac{rd\theta}{\sin \theta} \\ \frac{1}{r}dr &= 2 \cot \theta d \theta \\ \ln(r) &= 2 \ln(\abs{\sin \theta}) + C, \ \text{ where $C$ is aribtrary constant} \\ \ln(r) &= \ln(\sin^2 \theta) + C \\ e^{\ln(r)} &= e^{\ln(\sin^2 \theta) + C}\\ r &= e^C e^{\ln(sin^2 \theta)} = e^C \sin^2 \theta \\ \therefore r&= A \sin^2 \theta, \text{ where $A=e^C$ is constant}\\ \frac{dr}{2 \cos \theta} &= \frac{r \sin \theta d \psi}{0} \\ d \psi &= 0 \\ \psi &= \text{constant} \end{align*}$$

The equations of the streamlines are $$\begin{align*} \psi &= \text{ constant } \\ r &= A \sin^2 \theta \end{align*}$$
