Prove that the series $$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (-1)^n \frac{x^2+n}{n^2}$$ converges uniformly in every bounded interval, but does not converge absolutely for any value of $x$.

$$\begin{align*} \text{Let } c_n &= (-1)^n \frac{x^2+n}{n^2} = (-1)^n \bracket{\frac{x^2}{n^2}+ \frac{1}{n}} \\ \text{Then } c_{2m} &= (-1)^{2m} \frac{x^2+2m}{(2m)^2} \\ \text{and } c_{2m-1} &= (-1)^{2m-1} \frac{x^2+2m-1}{(2m-1)^2}, m=1,2,3,\cdots \\ \end{align*}$$

Then $c_{2m} > 0$ and $c_{2m-1} < 0$.

Since $\frac{x^2}{n^2} \geq \frac{x^2}{(n+1)^2}$ and $\frac{1}{n} > \frac{1}{n+1}$,
$\mod{c_1} \geq \mod{c_2} \geq \dots$

$$\limit c_n = \limit (-1)^n \bracket{\frac{x^2}{n^2}+ \frac{1}{n}} = 0$$

Thus by 3.43, the series converges for all $x$.

The partial sums are $$f_m(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{m} (-1)^n \bracket{\frac{x^2}{n^2}+ \frac{1}{n}} = x^2 \sum_{n=1}^{m} \frac{(-1)^n}{n^2} + \sum_{n=1}^{m} \frac{(-1)^n}{n} = x^2 A_m +B_m \to Ax^2+B$$

Let $\varepsilon > 0$. We choose $N$ such that $n \geq N$ implies $\mod{A_m-A} < \varepsilon$ and $\mod{B_m-B} < \varepsilon$

Let $[a,b]$ be a bounded interval and let $c=\max(\abs{a}, \abs{b})$

Then $n \geq N$ and $x \in [a,b]$ implies $\mod{f_n(x)-f(x)} < \ep c^2 + \varepsilon$

Hence $\{f_n\}$ converges uniformly to $f$ on $[a,b]$

But it does not coverge absolutely for any $x$ since $\summ \abs{c_n} = \summ \bracket{\frac{x^2}{n^2} + \frac{1}{n}} \geq \summ \frac{1}{n}$, which diverges.
