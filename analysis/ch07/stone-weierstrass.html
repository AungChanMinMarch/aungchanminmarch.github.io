If $f$ is a continuous complex function on $[a,b]$, there exists a sequence of polynomials $P_n$ such that $\limit P_n(x)=f(x)$ uniformly on $[a,b]$.

Without loss of generality, we may assume that $[a,b]=[0,1]$
We may also assume that $f(0) = f(1)=0$.
Furthermore, we define $f(x)$ to be zero for $x$ outside $[0,1]$.
Then $f$ is uniformly continuous on the whole line.

For every $\varepsilon >0$, there exist a $\delta > 0$ such that $\mod{y-x} < \delta$ implies that $\mod{f(y)-f(x)}<\frac{\varepsilon}{2}$

Put $\fQ_n(x) = c_n(1-x^2)^n$ for $n=1,2,3,\dots$, where $c_n$ is chosen so that $\int_{-1}^{1}\fQ_n(x)dx =1$ for $n=1,2,3, \dots$

Consider the function $(1-x^2)^n-1+nx^2$ which is zero at $x=0$ and whose derivative is $n(1-x^2)^{n-1}(-2x)+2nx =2nx(1-(1-x)^{n-1})$ is positive on $(0,1)$ because

\text{If } \ 0 &<& x &<& 1 \\ 0 &<& x^2 &<& 1 \\ 0 &>& -x^2 &>& -1 \\ 1 &>& 1-x^2 &>& 0 \\ 1 &>& (1-x^2)^{n-1} &>& 0 \\ -1 &<& -(1-x^2)^{n-1} &<& 0 \\ 0 &<& 1-(1-x^2)^{n-1} &<& 1 \\ 0 &<& 2nx(1-(1-x^2)^{n-1}) &<& 2nx
Therefore $(1-x^2)^n-1+nx^2 \geq 0$ and hence $(1-x^2)^n \geq 1-nx^2$.

$$\begin{align*} \int_{-1}^{1} (1-x^2)^n dx &= 2 \int_{0}^{1} (1-x^2)^n dx \\ &\geq 2 \int_{0}^{\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}} (1-x^2)^n dx \\ &\geq 2 \int_{0}^{\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}} (1-nx^2) dx\\ &= 2 \left[x-\frac{nx^3}{3}\right]_{0}^{\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}} \\ &= 2\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}-\frac{1}{3\sqrt{n}} \right) \\ &= \frac{4}{3\sqrt{n}} > \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \\ \int_{-1}^{1} (1-x^2)^n dx &> \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \\ \int_{-1}^{1} c_n (1-x^2)^n dx &> \frac{c_n}{\sqrt{n}} \\ 1&> \frac{c_n}{\sqrt{n}}\\ c_n &< \sqrt{n} \end{align*}$$

\text{If } \ \delta &\leq& x & \text{ and } \delta, x \leq 1 \\ \delta^2 &\geq& x^2 \\ -\delta^2 &\leq& -x^2 \\ 1-\delta^2 &\leq& 1-x^2 \\ (1-\delta^2)^n &\geq& (1-x^2)^n \\
$$\begin{align*} \therefore c_n(1-x^2)^n &< \sqrt{n}(1-\delta^2)^n & \text{ for } \delta \leq &\mod{x} \leq 1$\\ \fQ(x) &< \sqrt{n}(1-\delta^2)^n & \text{ for } \delta \leq &\mod{x} \leq 1$\\ \int_{\delta}^{1} \fQ(x) dx &\leq \end{align*}$$

Set $P_n(x)=\int_{-1}^{1}f(x+t) \mathcal{Q}_n(t) dt$, ($0 \leq \mod{x} \leq 1$)

$$\begin{align*} \text{If } 1-x < &t < 1, & \text{If } -1 < &t < -x, \\ 1 < t+&x < 1+x & x-1 < x&+t < 0 \\ f(t+x) &= 0 & f(t+x) &= 0\\ \int_{1-x}^{1} f(x+t) \mathcal{Q}_n(t) dt &=0 & \int_{-1}^{-x} f(x+t) \mathcal{Q}_n(t) dt &=0 \end{align*}$$ $$\therefore P_n(x)= \int_{-x}^{1-x} f(x+t) \mathcal{Q}_n(t) dt = \int_{0}^{1} f(t) \mathcal{Q}_n(t-x) dt = \int_{0}^{1} f(t)c_n(1-(t-x)^2)^n dt$$

Clearly, $P_n(x)$ is polynomial in $x$
Thus $\{P_n \}$ is a sequence of polynomials which are real if $f$ is real.

$$\begin{align*} \mod{P_n(x)-f(x)} &= \mod{\int_{-1}^{1} f(x+t)\fQ_n(t)dt -f(x) \int_{-1}^{1}\fQ_n(t)dt}\\ &= \mod{\int_{-1}^{1} (f(x+t)-f(x))\fQ_n(t)dt}\\ &\leq \int_{-1}^{1} \mod{f(x+t)-f(x)} \fQ_n(t) dt \\ &= \int_{-1}^{-\delta} \mod{f(x+t)-f(x)} \fQ_n(t) dt + \int_{-\delta}^{\delta} \mod{f(x+t)-f(x)} \fQ_n(t) dt + \int_{\delta}^{1} \mod{f(x+t)-f(x)} \fQ_n(t) dt \\ &= \end{align*}$$
