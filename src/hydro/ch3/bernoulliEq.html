Bernoulli Equation (page 96)

Derive the Bernoulli's equation in its most general form. What does this becomes for steady motion and for homogeneous and and incompressible fluid?

Suppose that the body forces are conservative and that the flow is of the potential kind. Then there exists scalar functions $\Omega, \varphi$ such that $$F= - \grad \Omega, \q = -\grad \varphi$$ Also $\curl q = \grad \times q = 0$.

Euler equation of motion is $\dfrac{dq}{dt} = F - \dfrac{1}{\rho} \grad p$.

Since $\dfrac{dq}{dt} = \dpartialwrt[q]{t} + \grad \bracket{\frac{1}{2}q^2} - q \times (\grad \times q)$, we have $$\begin{align*} \dpartialwrt[q]{t} + \grad \bracket{\frac{1}{2}q^2} - q \times (\grad \times q) &= F - \frac{1}{\rho} \grad p \\ \therefore \partialwrt{t}(-\grad \varphi) + \grad \bracket{\frac{1}{2}q^2} &= -\grad \Omega - \frac{1}{\rho} \grad p \\ -\grad \partialwrt[\varphi]{t} + \grad \bracket{\frac{1}{2}q^2} &= -\grad \Omega - \frac{1}{\rho} \grad p \\ \mbox{Scalar multiplying by } dr, \\ - dr . \grad \partialwrt[\varphi]{t} + dr . \grad \bracket{\frac{1}{2}q^2} &= - dr.\grad \Omega - \frac{1}{\rho} dr.\grad p \\ - d\bracket{\partialwrt[\varphi]{t}} + d\bracket{\frac{1}{2}q^2} &= -d \Omega - \frac{1}{\rho} dp\\ d\bracket{\frac{1}{2}q^2} + d \Omega + \frac{1}{\rho} dp - d\bracket{\partialwrt[\varphi]{t}} &= 0 \\ \mbox{Integrating gives } \\ \frac{1}{2}q^2 + \Omega + \int \frac{dp}{\rho} - \partialwrt[\varphi]{t} &= f(t), \mbox{where $f(t)$ is arbitrary function} \end{align*}$$ This is Bernoulli's equation in its most general form.

For steady motion $\dpartialwrt[\varphi]{t} = 0$ and $f(t)$ is constant so that $$\frac{1}{2}q^2 + \Omega + \int \frac{dp}{\rho} = \mbox{constant}$$

If, further, the fluid is homogeneous and incompressible, $\rho$ is constant. Hence $$\frac{1}{2}q^2 + \Omega + \frac{p}{\rho} = \mbox{constant}$$
