Definition: Continuous Function

Let $(X, \topologyTau)$ and $(Y, \topologyTau^*)$ be topological spaces. A function $f$ from $X$ into $Y$ is continuous relative to $\topologyTau$ and $\topologyTau^*$, or $\topologyTau-\topologyTau^*$ continuous, or simply continuous, iff the inverse image $f^{-1}[H]$ of every $\topologyTau^*$-open subset $H$ of $Y$ is a $\topologyTau$-open subset of X, that is, iff $$H \in \topologyTau^* \text{ implies } f^{-1}[H] \in \topologyTau$$

CONTINUOUS FUNCTIONS AND ARBITRARY CLOSENESS

Let $X$ be a topological space. A point $p \in X$ is said to be arbitrarily close to a set $A \subset X$ if either
  1. $p \in A$ or
  2. $p$ is an accumulation point of $A$

Eg 1.2 + Ex 2

  1. Eg 1.2: Consider any discrete space $(X, \discrete)$ and any topological space $(Y,\topologyTau)$. Then prove that every function $f: X \to Y$ is $\discrete-\topologyTau$ continuous.
  2. Ex 2: Prove: Let $f:X \to Y$ be any function. If $(Y, \indiscrete)$ is an indiscrete space, then $f:(X,\topologyTau) \to (Y, \indiscrete)$ is continuous for any $\topologyTau$.

  1. Since $(X, \discrete)$ is a discrete space, every subset of $X$ is open.

    Hence $f^{-1}[H]$ is open for any open set $H$ of $Y$.

    $f$ is continuous.

  2. Since $(Y, \indiscrete)$ is an indiscrete space, $Y$ and $\emptyset$ are the only open subsets of $Y$.

    $f^{-1}[Y] = X$ and $f^{-1}[\emptyset] = \emptyset$.

    $X$ and $\emptyset$ belong to any topology $\topologyTau$ on $X$.

    Hence $f$ is continuous for any $\topologyTau$.
