Acceleration of a Fluids

Take the Cartesian compoennts of $\vec{q}$ as $[u,v,w]$. Then the x-components of the acceleration is \dfrac{du}{dt}= & \dpartialwrt[u]{t} \dpartialwrt[t]{t} &+& \dpartialwrt[u]{x} \dpartialwrt[x]{t}& +& \dpartialwrt[u]{y} \dpartialwrt[y]{t} &+& \dpartialwrt[u]{z} \dpartialwrt[z]{t} \\ = & \dpartialwrt[u]{t} &+& \dpartialwrt[u]{x} u &+& \dpartialwrt[u]{y} v &+& \dpartialwrt[u]{z} w \\ = & \dpartialwrt[u]{t} &+& u \dpartialwrt[u]{x} &+& v \dpartialwrt[u]{y} &+& w \dpartialwrt[u]{z}\\ = & \dpartialwrt[u]{t} & +& (\vec{q} . \grad) u \\ \mbox{Similarly, } \dfrac{dv}{dt} =& \dpartialwrt[v]{t} & +& (\vec{q} . \grad) v \\ \mbox{and } \dfrac{dw}{dt} =& \dpartialwrt[w]{t} & +& (\vec{q} . \grad) w \\ \mbox{Hence } f=\dfrac{d \vec{q}}{dt} =& \dpartialwrt[\vec{q}]{t} &+& (\vec{q} . \grad)\vec{q}
