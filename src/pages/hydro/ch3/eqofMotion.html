Euler's Equation of Motion

At time $t$, suppose $\Delta S$ is the closed surface of a particle of a moving fluid.
Take the particle to be of fixed mass $\rho \Delta v$, where $\rho$ is the density and $\Delta v$ is the volume.
$\Delta S$ and $\Delta v$ are made vanishingly small.

Suppose that $\delta v$ is a volume element of $\Delta v$.
The mass of the volume element $\delta v$ is $\rho \delta v$.
Let $F$ be the body force per unit mass at any volume element $\delta v$.
The body force on the element $\delta v$ is $F \rho \delta v$.
The total body force is $\int_{\Delta v} F \rho dv$.

Suppose that $\delta S$ is a surface element of $\Delta S$.
Let $\hat{n}$ be the unit normal vector to $\Delta S$, drawn outwards from the particle.
Let $p$ denote the fulid pressure at $\delta S$.
The force due to the fluid outside the particle is $-p \delta S \hat{n}= - \hat{n} p \delta S$
Hence the total surface force is $-\int_{\Delta S} \hat{n} p dS = - \int_{\Delta v} \grad p dv$

The net force is $\int_{\Delta v} F \rho dv - \int_{\Delta v} \grad p dv = \int_{\Delta v} (F \rho - \grad p) dv$
Let $\vec{q}$ be the velocity of fluid particle.
Since the mass of $\delta v$ is $\rho \delta v$, the mass of the entire fluid is $\int_{\Delta v} \rho dv$.
Since this mass is constant, the equation of motion is $$ \begin{align*} \int_{\Delta v} (F \rho - \grad p) dv &= \frac{d\vec{q}}{dt} \int_{\Delta v} \rho dv\\ (F \rho - \grad p) \Delta v &= \frac{d\vec{q}}{dt} \rho \Delta v \\ F \rho - \grad p &= \frac{d\vec{q}}{dt} \rho \\ \frac{d\vec{q}}{dt} &= F - \frac{1}{\rho} \grad p \end{align*}$$
