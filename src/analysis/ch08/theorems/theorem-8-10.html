--- title: "Definition 8.10 : Orthogonal and orthonormal" order: 10 ---

Let $\{ \phi_n \}$ ($n=1,2,3, \dots$) be a sequence of complex functions on $[a,b]$, such that $$\int_a^b \phi_n(x) \conj{\phi_m(x)} dx = 0, \text{ where } n \neq m$$ Then $\{ \phi_n \}$ is said to be an orthogonal system of functions on $[a,b]$. If, in addition, $$\int_a^b \abs{\phi_n(x)}^2 dx = 1$$ for all $n$, then $\{ \phi_n \}$ is said to be orthonormal

Prove that the functions $(2 \pi)^{-\frac{1}{2}} e^{inx}$ form an orthonormal system on $[-\pi, \pi]$

For $n \neq m$, $$\begin{align} &\int\limits_{-\pi}^{\pi} (2\pi)^{-\frac{1}{2}} e^{inx} \conj{(2\pi)^{-\frac{1}{2}} e^{imx}} dx \\ =& \int\limits_{-\pi}^{\pi} (2\pi)^{-\frac{1}{2}} e^{inx} (2\pi)^{-\frac{1}{2}} e^{-imx} dx \\ =& \int\limits_{-\pi}^{\pi} 2\pi e^{i(n-m)x} dx \\ =& \frac{1}{2\pi} \int\limits_{-\pi}^{\pi} e^{i(n-m)x} dx \\ =& 0 \end{align}$$ For any integer $n$, $$\begin{align} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \abs{(2\pi)^{-\frac{1}{2}} e^{inx}}^2 dx &= \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} (2\pi)^{-1} \abs{e^{inx}}^2 dx \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} (1)^2 dx \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi} (2\pi) \\ &= 1 \end{align}$$

Hence the functions $(2 \pi)^{-\frac{1}{2}} e^{inx}$ form an orthonormal system on $[-\pi, \pi]$

Prove that the real functions $$\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}, \frac{\cos x}{\sqrt{\pi}}, \frac{\sin x}{\sqrt{\pi}}, \frac{\cos 2x}{\sqrt{\pi}}, \frac{\sin 2x}{\sqrt{\pi}},\cdots, $$ form an orthonormal system on $[-\pi, \pi]$

$$\begin{align} \text{For any integer } n, \\ \int_{-pi}^{\pi} \cos nx dx &= \sbracket{\frac{\sin nx}{n}}_{x=-\pi}^{\pi} \\ &= \frac{\sin n\pi}{n} - \frac{\sin (-n\pi)}{n} \\ &= 0 - 0 =0 \\ \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin nx dx &= \sbracket{-\frac{\cos nx}{n}}_{x=-\pi}^{\pi} \\ &= -\frac{\cos n\pi}{n} + \frac{\cos (-n\pi)}{n} \\ &= -\frac{\cos n\pi}{n} + \frac{\cos n\pi}{n} \\ &= 0 \end{align}$$

Hence $\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos nx dx =0$ and $\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin nx dx =0$ for any integer $n$.

$$\begin{align} &\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos nx \cos mx dx \\ =& \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \frac{\cos (nx + mx) + \cos (nx -mx)}{2} dx \\ =& \frac{1}{2} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos (n+m)x + \cos (n-m)x dx \\ =& 0 \end{align}$$ $$\begin{align} &\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin nx \sin mx dx \\ =& \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \frac{\cos (nx + mx) - \cos (nx -mx)}{2} dx \\ =& \frac{1}{2} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos (n+m)x - \cos (n-m)x dx \\ =& 0 \end{align}$$ $$\begin{align} &\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos nx \sin mx dx \\ =& \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \frac{\sin (nx + mx) - \sin (nx -mx)}{2} dx \\ =& \frac{1}{2} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin (n+m)x - \sin (n-m)x dx \\ =& 0 \end{align}$$

Any two random functions of the given functions are one of the forms: $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \frac{\cos nx}{\sqrt{\pi}}$ or $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \frac{\sin nx}{\sqrt{\pi}}$ or $\frac{\cos nx}{\sqrt{\pi}} \frac{\sin mx}{\sqrt{\pi}}$. But integrating them from $-\pi$ to $\pi$ is zero. Hence they form orthogonal system.

$$\begin{align} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \abs{\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}}^2 dx &= \int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\frac{1}{2\pi} dx \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi} [x]_{-\pi}^{\pi} \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi} (2\pi) \\ &= 1 \end{align}$$ $$\begin{align} \text{For } n=1,2,3,\cdots,\\ \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \abs{\frac{\cos nx}{\sqrt{\pi}}}^2 dx &= \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \frac{\cos^2 nx}{\pi} dx \\ &= \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \frac{1+\cos 2nx}{2\pi} dx \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi} \bracket{\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} dx + \int_{-pi}^{\pi} \cos 2nx dx} \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi} \bracket{ [x]_{-\pi}^{\pi} + 0} \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi} (2\pi) \\ &= 1 \end{align}$$ $$\begin{align} \text{For } n=1,2,3,\cdots,\\ \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \abs{\frac{\sin nx}{\sqrt{\pi}}}^2 dx &= \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \frac{\sin^2 nx}{\pi} dx \\ &= \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \frac{1-\cos 2nx}{2\pi} dx \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi} \bracket{\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} dx - \int_{-pi}^{\pi} \cos 2nx dx} \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi} \bracket{ [x]_{-\pi}^{\pi} - 0} \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi} (2\pi) \\ &= 1 \end{align}$$

Hence there functions form orthonormal system on $[-\pi, \pi]$.

If $\{ \phi_n \}$ is orthonormal on $[a, b]$ and if $$c_n = \int\limits_{a}^{b} f(t) \conj{\phi_n(t)} dt (n = 1, 2, 3, ... ), \tag{66}$$ we call $c_n$ the nth Fourier coefficient of $f$ relative to $\{ \phi_n \}$. We write $$ f(x) \sim \sum_{1}^{\infty} c_n \phi_n(x) \tag{67}$$ and call this series the Fourier series of $f$ (relative to $\{\phi_n\}$).

Note that the symbol $\sim$ used in (67) implies nothing about the convergence of the series; it merely says that the coefficients are given by (66).
