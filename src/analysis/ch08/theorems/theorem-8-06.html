Let $e^x$ be defined on $R^1$ by $x^x = E(x) \summ \frac{x^n}{n!}$.

(a) $e^x$ is continuous and differentiable for all $x$.
(b) $(e^x)' = e^x$
$$\begin{align} \lim\limits_{h \to 0} \frac{E(z+h) -E(z)}{h} &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{E(z)E(h) - E(z)}{h} \\ &= E(z) \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{E(h) - 1}{h} \\ &= E(z) \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1+ h + \frac{h^2}{2!} + \frac{h^3}{3!} +\dots - 1}{h}\\ &= E(z) \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{h + \frac{h^2}{2!} + \frac{h^3}{3!} +\dots}{h}\\ &= E(z) \lim_{h \to 0} 1 + \frac{h}{2!} + \frac{h^2}{3!} + \dots\\ &= E(z)(1+ 0+ 0 + \dots) \\ &= E(z) \end{align}$$

Hence $(e^x)'$ exists and equal to $e^x$. Therefore $e$ is differentiable and hence by {% a 5.2 %}, it is continuous for all $x$.

(c) $e^x$ is a strictly increasing function of $x$ and $e^x > 0$

Since $e^x = \summ \frac{x^n}{n!}$, $0 < x < y$ implies that $E(x) < E(y)$. Therefore $e^x$ is strictly increasing for all positive $x$.

$$\begin{align} E(x) & < E(y) \\ \frac{1}{E(x)} & > \frac{1}{E(y)} \\ E(-x) & > E(-y) \end{align}$$ Hence $e^x$ is a strictly increasing function of $x$. Since $e^x = \summ \frac{x^n}{n!}$, $e^x > 0$ if $x>0$.
Since $E(x) E(-x) = E(x+(-x)) = E(0) = 1$, $E(-x) = \frac{1}{E(x)} > 0$ since $E(x) >0$.
Therefore $e^x > 0$ for all real $x$.
(d) $e^{x+y} = e^x e^y$

By using {% a '3.50' %}, we have $$\begin{align} E(z)E(w) &= \summ \frac{z^n}{n!} \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \frac{w^m}{m!} \\ &= \summ \sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{z^k}{k!} \frac{w^{n-k}}{(n-k)!} \\ &= \summ \sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{1}{(n-k)!k!} z^k w^{n-k}\\ &= \summ \sum_{k=0}^{n}\frac{n!}{n!} \frac{1}{(n-k)!k!} z^k w^{n-k}\\ &= \summ \frac{1}{n!} \sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{n!}{(n-k)!k!} z^k w^{n-k}\\ &= \summ \frac{1}{n!} \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} z^k w^{n-k}\\ &= \summ \frac{1}{n!} (z+w)^n \\ &= \summ \frac{(z+w)^n}{n!} \\ &= E(z+w)\\ \therefore e^x e^y &= e^{x+y} \end{align}$$

(e) $e^x \to + \infty$ as $x \to + \infty$ and $e^x \to 0$ as $ x \to - \infty$

Since $e^x = \summ \frac{x^n}{n!} = 1 + x + \frac{x^2}{2!} + \frac{x^3}{3!} + \dots$, $e^x \to \infty$ as $x \to \infty$

$$\begin{align} e^{x+(-x)} &= e^x e^{-x} \\ e^0 &= e^x e^{-x} \\ 1 &= e^x e^{-x} \\ e^{-x} &= \frac{1}{e^x} \end{align}$$ Hence $e^{-x} \to 0$ as $x \to \infty$.
(f) $\lim\limits_{x \to +\infty} x^n e^{-x} = 0$ for every $n$.

Since $e^x = \summ \frac{x^n}{n!} = 1 + x + \frac{x^2}{2!} + \frac{x^3}{3!} + \dots$, $e^x \to \infty$ as $x \to \infty$

$$\begin{align} e^x > \frac{x^{n+1}}{(n+1)!} \\ e^{-x} &< \frac{(n+1)!}{x^{n+1}} \\ x^n e^{-x} &< \frac{(n+1)!}{x} \\ \end{align}$$

Since $\lim\limits_{x \to +\infty} \frac{(n+1)!}{x} = 0$ for every $n$, $\lim\limits_{x \to +\infty} x^n e^{-x} = 0$ for every $n$.

(A), (C) Since $E$ is increasing and differentiable on $R^1$, it has an inverse function $L$ which is also strictly increasing and differentiable and whose domain is $E(R^1)$, that is, the set of all positive numbers. $L$ is defined by $$E(L(y)) = y (y >0)$$ or equivalently, by $$L(E(x))= x (x \in R)$$
(B) $L'(y) = \frac{1}{y} (y > 0)$
Since $L(E(x)) = x$, differentiating both sides with respect to $x$ gives $$\begin{align} L'(E(x)) E(x) &= 1 \\ L'(E(x)) &= \frac{1}{E(x)} \\ \text{Set } y &= E(X), \\ L'(y) &= \frac{1}{y} \end{align}$$ $$\begin{align} \text{Since } L(E(x)) &= x,\\ \text{taking } x &= 0,\\ L(E(0)) &= 0 \\ L(1) &= 0 \end{align}$$ Since $L'(y) = \frac{1}{y}$, integrating both sides from $1$ to $t$ gives $$\begin{align} \int_{1}^{t} L'(y) dy &= \int_{1}^{t} \frac{1}{y} dy \\ L(t) - L(1) &= \int_{1}^{t} \frac{1}{x} dx \\ L(t) &= \int_{1}^{t} \frac{1}{x} dx \\ L(y) &= \int_{1}^{y} \frac{1}{x} dx \\ \end{align}$$
(D) $L(uv) = L(u) + L(v)$ for $u >0, v>0$
Let $u=E(x)$ and $v=E(y)$. Then $L(u) = L(E(x)) = x$ and $L(v) = L(E(y)) = y$. $$\begin{align} L(uv) &= L(E(x)E(y)) \\ &= L(E(x+y)) \\ &= x+y \\ &= L(u) + L(v) \end{align}$$
(E) $\log x \to +\infty$ as $x \to + \infty$ and $\log x \to - \infty$ as $x \to 0$
(F) $\lim\limits_{x \to +\infty} x^{-a} \log x = 0$
For if $0 < \ep < \alpha$ and $x > 1$, $$\begin{align} x^{-\alpha} \log x &= x^{- \alpha} \int_{1}^{x} \frac{1}{t} dt \\ &< x^{-\alpha} t^{\ep} \int_{1}^{x} \frac{1}{t} dt \\ &= x^{-\alpha} \int_{1}^{x} t^{\ep -1} dt \\ &= x^{-\alpha} \sbracket{\frac{t^{\ep}}{\ep}}_{t=1}^{x}\\ &= x^{-\alpha} \bracket{\frac{x^{\ep}}{\ep} - \frac{1}{\ep}} \\ &< x^{-\alpha} \frac{x^{\ep}}{\ep} = \frac{1}{\ep x^{\alpha-\ep}} \\ \end{align}$$ Since $\lim\limits_{x \to +\infty} \frac{1}{\ep x^{\alpha - \ep}} = 0 $,
$\lim\limits_{x \to +\infty} x^{-a} \log x = 0$.
