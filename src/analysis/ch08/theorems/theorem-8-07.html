(a) The function $E$ is periodic, with period $2\pi i$.

First we shall prove there is positive numbers $x$ such that $C(x) = 0$ by using contradiction method. Assume there is no $x > 0$ such that $C(x) = 0$. Then $C(x) \neq 0$ for all $x > 0$.

Since $C(0) =1$, $C(x) > 0$ for all $x >0 $.

Since $S'(x) = C(x) > 0$ for all $x > 0$, $S$ is strictly increasing for $x > 0$.

Since $S(0) = 0$, $S(x) > 0 $ for all $x > 0$.

\begin{align} S(x) (y-x) &< \int_{x}^{y} S(t) dt \\ &= [-C(t)]_{x}^{y} \\ &= -C(y) + C(x) \\ &\leq \abs{-C(y) + C(x)} \\ &\leq \abs{C(y)} + \abs{C(x)} \\ &= \sqrt{C^2(y)} + \sqrt{C^2(x)} \\ &\leq \sqrt{C^2(y) + S^2(y)} + \sqrt{C^2(x) + S^2(x)} \\ &= \abs{E(iy)} + \abs{E(ix)} \\ &= 1 + 1 = 2\\ S(x) &< \frac{2}{y-x} \end{align}

Thus $S(x) < \frac{2}{y-x} \to 0$ if $y$ is large enough. This contradicts that $S(x) > 0$ for all $x > 0$. Our assumption is wrong. There are positive numbers $x$ such that $C(x) = 0$

Let $x_0$ be the smallest positive number such that $C(x_0) = 0$ and define the number $\pi = 2x_0$.

\begin{align} C(\frac{\pi}{2}) &= C(x_0) = 0 \\ E\bracket{ i\frac{\pi}{2} } &= C(\frac{\pi}{2}) + i S(\frac{\pi}{2}) \\ E \bracket{ i\frac{\pi}{2} } &= i S(\frac{\pi}{2}) \\ \abs{E(i\frac{\pi}{2})} &= \sqrt{C^(\frac{\pi}{2}) + S^2(\frac{\pi}{2})} \\ 1 &= \sqrt{S^2(\frac{\pi}{2})}\\ 1 &= S^2(\frac{\pi}{2})\\ S(\frac{\pi}{2}) &= \pm 1 \end{align}

Since $S'(x) = C(x) > 0$ in $(0, x_0)$, $S$ is increasing in $(0, x_0)$. Thus $S(x_0) = S(\frac{\pi}{2}) = -1$ is impossible and $S(x_0) = i$ \begin{align} E(i\frac{\pi}{2}) &= i S(\frac{\pi}{2}) = i \\ E(i\pi) &= E\bracket{ \frac{i\pi}{2} + \frac{i\pi}{2} } = E\bracket{i\frac{\pi}{2}} E\bracket{i\frac{\pi}{2}}= (i)(i) = -1 \\ E(2\pi i) &= E(\pi i + \pi) i = E(\pi i)E(\pi i) = (-1)(-1) = 1 \\ \end{align} $$E(x+ 2\pi i) = E(x) E(2 \pi i) = E(x)$$

Thus $E$ is periodic function with period $2 \pi i$

The functions $C$ and $S$ are periodic with period $2 \pi$.

\begin{align} C(z) &= \frac{1}{2} [E(iz) + E(-iz)] \\ C(z + 2\pi i) &= \frac{1}{2} [E(iz + 2\pi i) + E(-iz + 2 \pi i)] \\ &= \frac{1}{2} [E(iz) + E(-iz)] \\ &= C(z) \end{align}

Thus $C$ is periodic with period $2\pi$.

\begin{align} S(z) &= \frac{1}{2i} [E(iz) - E(-iz)] \\ S(z + 2\pi i) &= \frac{1}{2i} [E(iz + 2\pi i) - E(-iz + 2 \pi i)] \\ &= \frac{1}{2i} [E(iz) - E(-iz)] \\ &= S(z) \end{align}

Thus $S$ is periodic with period $2\pi$.

If $z$ is a complex number with $\abs{z} = 1$, there is a unique $t$ in $[0, 2\pi)$ such that $E(it) =z$.

We need to prove existence and uniqueness of $t$ in $[0, 2\pi)$ such that $E(it) =z$.

First we shall prove its existence.

Put $z= x+ iy$, where $x$ and $y$ are real. Then $\abs{z} = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$.

First suppose $x \geq 0$ and $y>0$.

On $[0, \frac{\pi}{2}]$, $C$ decreases from $1$ to $0$. Hence $C(t) =x$ for some $t \in [0, \frac{\pi}{2}]$. Since $C^2 + S^2 =1$ and $S \geq 0$ on $\frac{\pi}{2}]$, $$E(it) = C(t) + i S(t) = x + iy = z$$

Therefore if $z=x+iy$ is a complex number with $\abs{z} = 1$, $x \geq 0$ and $y \geq 0$, there is a $t$ in $[0, \frac{\pi}{2}]$ such that $E(it) =z$. (a)

Now suppose $x < 0$ and $y \geq 0$.

Then $-iz = -i(x+iy) =y + i(-x)$

Since $x < 0$, we have $-x > 0$.

Hence $-iz=y+i(-x)$ is a complex number with $\abs{-iz} = \abs{z} = 1$, $-x \geq 0$ and $y \geq 0$. By statement (a), there is $t$ in $[0, \frac{\pi}{2}]$ such that $E(it) = -iz$.

\begin{align} E(it) &= -iz \\ iE(it) &= z \\ E(i\frac{\pi}{2})E(it) &= z \\ E(i\frac{\pi}{2} + it) &= z \\ E\bracket{i(\frac{\pi}{2} + t)} &= z \\ \end{align}

$t \in [0, \frac{\pi}{2}]$ implies $t + \frac{\pi}{2} \in [\frac{\pi}{2}, \pi]$.

Therefore if $z=x+iy$ is a complex number with $\abs{z} = 1$, $x < 0$ and $y \geq 0$, there is $t$ in $[\frac{\pi}{2}, \pi]$ such that $E(it) =z$. (b)

By statements (a) and (b), we get the following new statements.

If $z=x+iy$ is a complex number with $\abs{z} = 1$ and $y \geq 0$, there is $t$ in $[0, \pi]$ such that $E(it) =z$. (c)

Finally suppose that $y < 0$.

Then $-z = -x + i(-y)$ is a $z=x+iy$ is a complex number with $\abs{-z} = \abs{z} = 1$ and $-y \geq 0$.

By statement (c), there is $t$ in $[0, \pi)$ such that $E(it) = -z$

\begin{align} E(it) &= -z \\ (-1)E(it) &= z \\ E(i\pi)E(it) &= z \\ E(i \pi + it) &= z \\ E\bracket{i(\pi + t)} &= z \\ \end{align}

$t \in [0, \frac{\pi}{2})$ implies $\pi + t \in [\pi, 2\pi)$.

Therefore if $z=x+iy$ is a complex number with $\abs{z} = 1$, $y < 0$, there is $t$ in $[\pi, 2\pi)$ such that $E(it) =z$. (d)

By statements (a) and (b), if $z=x+iy$ is a complex number with $\abs{z} = 1$, there is $t$ in $[0, 2\pi)$ such that $E(it) = z$. (c)

Now we shall prove its uniqueness.
