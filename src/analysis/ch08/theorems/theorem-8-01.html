Suppose the series $$\summ c_n x^n \tag{3}$$ converges for $\abs{x} < R$, and define $f(x)=\summ c_n x^n$ ($\abs{x} < R$).

Then (3) converges uniformly on $[-R+\ep, R- \ep ]$, no matter which $\ep > 0$ is chosen. The function $f$ is continuous and differentiable in $(-R,R)$ and $f'(x) = \sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty} n c_n x^{n-1} (\abs{x} < R)$

Let $\ep > 0$ be given.

$$\begin{align} \text{If } x &\in [-R + \ep, R - \ep], \\ \abs{x} &\leq R - \ep \\ \abs{x}^n &\leq \abs{R - \ep}^n \\ \abs{c_n} \abs{x^n} &\leq \abs{c_n} \abs{R - \ep}^n \\ \abs{c_n x^n} &\leq \abs{c_n (R - \ep)^n} \end{align}$$

$$\begin{align} \limit \sup \sqrt[n]{\abs{c_n(R-\ep)^n}} &= \limit \sup ( \abs{c_n}^{\frac{1}{n}} (R-\ep)) \\ &= \alpha (R - \ep)\\ &= \frac{1}{R} (R-\ep)\\ &= 1 - \frac{\ep}{R} < 1 \end{align}$$

Therefore $\summ c_n (R - \ep)^n$ converges.

Since $\abs{R- \ep} < R$ and $\summ c_n x^n$ converges for $\abs{x} < R$, $\summ c_n (R - \ep)^n$ converges.

Hence, by {% a '7.10' %}, $\summ c_n x^n$ converges uniformly on $[-R+\ep, R- \ep ]$.

$$ \begin{align} \limit \sqrt[n]{n} &= \limit e^{\ln{\sqrt[n]{n}}} \\ &= \limit e^{\frac{1}{n} \ln{n}} \\ &= e^{\limit \frac{\ln n}{n}} \bracket{\text{indeterminate form } \frac{\infty}{\infty}} \\ &= e^{\limit \frac{\frac{1}{n}}{1}} \\ &= e^{0} \\ &= 1 \end{align}$$ $$\limit \sup \sqrt[n]{n \abs{c_n}} = \limit \sup \sqrt[n]{n} \sqrt[n]{\abs{c_n}} = \limit \sup \sqrt[n]{\abs{c_n}}$$

Hence the series $\sum c_n x^{n}$ and $\sum nc_n x^{n-1}$ have the same interval of convergence. In other words, the series $\sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty} nc_nx^{n-1}$ converges for $\abs{x} < R$. So it converges uniformly on $[-R+\ep, R-\ep]$ for every $\ep > 0$.

By {% a 7.17 %}, $f'(x) = \sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty} n c_n x^{n-1}$ for $\abs{x} \leq R - \ep$

But, for any $\abs{x} \in R$, we can find $\ep > 0$ such that $\abs{x} < R - \ep$. Hence $f'(x) = \sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty} n c_n x^{n-1}$ for $\abs{x} < R$.

By {% a 5.2 %} , $f'$ is continuous.

Corollary

Under the hypeotheses of Theorem 8.1, $f$ has derivatives of all orders in $(-R,R)$, which are given by $$f^{(k)}(x) = \sum\limits_{n=k}^{\infty} n(n-1) \cdots (n-k+1) c_n x^{n-k}$$ In particular, $$f^{(k)}(0) = k! c_k (k=0,1,2,\dots).$$ (Here $f^{(0)}$ means $f$ and $f^{(k)}$ is the kth derivative of $f$ for $k=1,2,3,\dots$.)

If $f(x) = \summ c_n x^n$ converges for each $x \in (-R, R)$, then by Theorem 8.1, it converges uniformly on $[-R+ \ep, R- \ep]$ for any $\ep > 0$ and $f'(x) = \sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty} n c_n x^{n-1}$.

Since $\limit \sqrt[n]n = 1$, $\limit \sqrt[n]{n\abs{c_n}} = \sqrt[n]{\abs{c_n}}$. Hence $f$ and $f'$ have the same interval of convergence. In other words, $f'(x)$ converges on $[-R+ \ep, R- \ep]$ for any $\ep > 0$

For any $x \in (-R,R)$, we can find $\ep >0$ such that $x \in [-R+\ep, R-\ep]$. Hence $f'(x)$ converges on $(-R,R)$.

Similarly, since $f'(x) = \sum nc_n x^{n-1}$ converges for each $x \in (-R, R)$, then by Theorem 8.1, $f''(x) = \sum\limits_{n=2}^{\infty} n(n-1) c_n x^{n-2}$.

Therefore $f$ has derivatives of all orders in $(-R, R)$ and given by

$$\begin{align} f'(x) &= \sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty} n c_n x^{n-1} (x \in (-R, R))\\ &= c_1 + 2 c_2 x + 3 c_3 x^2 + 4 c_4 x^3 + \dots \\ f''(x) &= 2c_2 + 3.2 c_3 x + 4.3 c_4 x^2 + 5.4 c_5 x^3 + \dots \\ f'''(x) &= 3.2 c_3 + 4.3.2 c_4 x + 5.4.3 c_5 x^2 \\ &+ 6.5.4 c_6 x^3 + \dots\\ f^{(4)} &= 4.3.2.1 c_4 + 5.2.3.2 c_5 x +6.5.4.3 c_6 x^2\\ &+ 7.6.5.4 c_7 x^3 + \dots \\ \end{align}

Note that $$\begin{align} f'(x) &= \sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty} n c_n x^{n-1} (x \in (-R, R))\\ f''(x) &= 2c_2 + 3.2 c_3 x + 4.3 c_4 x^2 + 5.4 c_5 x^3 + \dots \\ f^{(2)} &= \sum\limits_{n=2}^{\infty} n(n-1) c_n x^{n-2} \\ f'''(x) &= 3.2 c_3 + 4.3.2 c_4 x + 5.4.3 c_5 x^2\\ &+ 6.5.4 c_6 x^3 + \dots\\ f^{(3)} &= \sum\limits_{n=3}^{\infty} n(n-1)(n-2) c_n x^{n-3} \\ f^{(4)} &= 4.3.2.1 c_4 + 5.2.3.2 c_5 x +6.5.4.3 c_6 x^2\\ &+ 7.6.5.4 c_7 x^3 + \dots \\ f^{(4)} &= \sum\limits_{n=4}^{\infty} n(n-1)(n-2)(n-3) c_n x^{n-4} \\ \text{In genreal, }\\ f^{(k)} &= \sum\limits_{n=k}^{\infty} n(n-1)\dots(n-k+1) c_n x^{n-k} \\ \end{align}$$

In particular,

$$\begin{align} f^{(1)}(0) &= c_1 &&= 1!c_1 \\ f^{(2)}(0) &= 2.1 c_2 &&= 2!c_2 \\ f^{(3)}(0) &= 3.2.1 c_3 &&= 3!c_3 \\ f^{(4)}(0) &= 4.3.2.1 c_4 &&= 4!c_4 \\ \end{align}$$

In general, $f^{(k)}(0) = k!c_k$
