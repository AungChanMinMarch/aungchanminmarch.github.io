--- title: "Theorem 8.8" parent: algebricCompletenessOfComplexField08 order: 8 ---
Suppose $a_0, \dots, a_n$ are complex numbers. $n\geq 1$, $a_n \neq 0$ $$P(z) = \sum_{k=0}^n a_k z^k$$ Then $P(z) = 0$ for some complex number $z$.

Without loss of generality, assume $a_n = 1$.

Put $\mu = \inf \abs{P(z)}$, where $z$ is a complex number.

$$\begin{align} &\text{If } \abs{z} = R, \text{ then} \\ &\abs{P(z)}\\ =& \abs{z^n + a_{n-1} z^{n-1} + a_{n-2} z^{n-2} + \dots + a_1 z + a_0} \\ \geq &\abs{z^n} - \bracket{\abs{ a_{n-1} z^{n-1} + a_{n-2} z^{n-2} + \dots + a_1 z + a_0 }}\\ \geq &\abs{z^n} - \bracket{ \abs{ a_{n-1} z^{n-1}} + \abs{a_{n-2} z^{n-2}} + \dots + \abs{a_1 z} + a_0}\\ = &R^n - ( \abs{ a_{n-1}} R^{n-1} + \abs{a_{n-2}} R^{n-2} + \dots + \abs{a_1} R + a_0)\\ =& R^n \bracket{1 - \abs{ a_{n-1}} R^{-1} - \abs{a_{n-2}} R^{-2} + \dots + a_0R^{-n}} \end{align}$$ The right side of this equation tends to $\infty$ as $R \to \infty$,

Hence there exists $R_0$ such that $\abs{P(z)} > \mu$ if $\abs{z} > R_0$. Since $\abs{P}$ is continuous on the closed disc with center at $0$ and radius $R_0$, {% a 4.16 %} shows that $\abs{P(z_0)} = \mu$ for some $z_0$.

Suppose $\mu \neq 0$ and put $Q(z) = \frac{P(z+z_0)}{P(z_0)}$

Then $Q(0) = \frac{P(0+z_0)}{P(z_0)} = 1$ and $\abs{Q(z)} = \abs{\frac{P(z+z_0)}{P(z_0)}} \geq 1$ for all $z$.

Hence $Q$ is a nonconstant polynomial. There exists a smallest integer $k$, $1 \leq k \leq n$ such that $Q(z) = 1 + b_k z^k + \dots + b_n z^n$, where $b_k \neq 0$.

Since $-\frac{\abs{b_k}}{b_k}$ is a complex number and $\abs{-\frac{\abs{b_k}}{b_k}} = 1$, by {% a '8.7' %} (d), there is a real $\theta$ such that $e^{ik \theta} = - \dfrac{\abs{b_k}}{b_k}$.
Hence $b_k e^{ik \theta} = - \abs{b_k}$.

For $0 < r < \frac{1}{\sqrt[k]{\abs{b_k}}}$, we have $r > 0$ and $r^k \abs{b_k} < 1$

$$\abs{1+b_k r^k e^{ik \theta} } = \abs{1 - r^k \abs{b_k}} = 1 - r^k \abs{b_k}$$ $$\begin{align} &\abs{Q(re^{i \theta})} \\ =& \abs{1 + b_k (r e^{i \theta})^k + b_{k+1} (r e^{i \theta})^{k+1} + \dots + b_n (r e^{i \theta})^n} \\ =&\abs{1 + b_k r^k e^{ik \theta} + b_{k+1} r^{k+1} e^{i(k+1) \theta} + \dots + b_n r^n e^{in \theta}} \\ \leq&\abs{1 + b_k r^k e^{ik \theta}} +\abs{b_{k+1} r^{k+1} e^{i(k+1) \theta}} + \dots + \abs{b_n r^n e^{in \theta}} \\ = & 1 - r^k \abs{b_k} + \abs{b_{k+1}} r^{k+1} + \dots + \abs{b_n} r^n \\ = & 1 - r^k \bracket{ \abs{b_k} - \abs{b_{k+1}} r - \dots - \abs{b_n} r^{n-k}} \end{align}$$

If $r$ is sufficient small, $\abs{b_{k+1}} r + \dots + \abs{b_n} r^{n-k}$ will be less than $\abs{b_k}$.

$$\begin{align} \abs{b_{k+1}} r + \dots + \abs{b_n} r^{n-k} & < \abs{b_k} \\ - \abs{b_{k+1}} r - \dots - \abs{b_n} r^{n-k} & > -\abs{b_k} \\ \abs{b_k} - \abs{b_{k+1}} r - \dots - \abs{b_n} r^{n-k} & > 0\\ r^k (\abs{b_k} - \abs{b_{k+1}} r - \dots - \abs{b_n} r^{n-k}) & > 0\\ - r^k (\abs{b_k} - \abs{b_{k+1}} r - \dots - \abs{b_n} r^{n-k}) & < 0\\ 1 - r^k (\abs{b_k} - \abs{b_{k+1}} r - \dots - \abs{b_n} r^{n-k}) & < 1\\ \abs{Q(z)} & < 1 \end{align}$$

This contradicts that $Q(z) \geq 1$ for all $z$.

Thus $\mu = 0$ and $P(z_0) = 0$
