Define $$f(x) = \begin{cases} e^{-\frac{1}{x^2}} &(x \neq 0)\\ 0 &(x =0) \end{cases}$$ Prove that $f$ has derivative of all orders at $x=0$, and that $f^{(n)}(0) = 0$ for $n=1,2,3,\dots$.

Let $y=\frac{1}{x}$. Then $f(x) = e^{-y^2}$ for $x \neq 0$. Let $g(y) = e^{-y^2}$. Then

$$\begin{align} g'(y) &= -2ye^{-y^2} \\ g''(y) &= -2e^{-y^2} + 4y^2 e^{-y^2} \\ &= (4y^2 - 2) e^{-y^2} \\ \vdots \\ g^{(n)}(y) &= P_n(y)e^{-y^2},\\ &\text{ where } P_n(y) \text{ is some $n$-order polynomial of $y$, }\\ &\text{for }n=1,2,3,\dots \end{align}$$

Now we shall prove that $f^{(n)}(0)=0$ using induction. For $n=1$,

$$\begin{align} f^{(1)}(0) &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(h) - f(0)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{e^{-\frac{1}{h^2}} - 0}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\frac{1}{h}}{e^{\frac{1}{h^2}}} \\ &\text{letting } t=\frac{1}{h}\\ &= \lim_{t \to \infty} \frac{t}{e^{t^2}} \bracket{\text{indeterminate form: } \frac{\infty}{\infty}} \\ &= \lim_{t \to \infty} \frac{1}{2te^{t^2}} \\ &= 0 \end{align}$$

Suppose $f^{(k)}(0) = 0$.

$$\begin{align} f^{(k+1)} &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f^{(k)}(h) - f^{(k)}(0)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{y \to \infty} y g^{(k)}(y) \\ &= \lim_{y \to \infty} y e^{-y^2} P_k(y) \\ &= \lim_{y \to \infty} y e^{-y^2} \lim_{y \to \infty} P_k(y) \\ &= 0 \end{align}$$ Therefore $f^{(n)}(0) = 0$ for all $n=1,2,3, \dots$.
