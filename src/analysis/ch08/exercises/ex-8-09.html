--- title: "ex no 9 (chapter 8)" order: 8 ---

Put $s_N = 1 + \frac{1}{2} + \cdots+ \dots + \frac{1}{N}$. Prove that $$\lim_{N \to \infty} (s_N - \log N)$$ exists. {The limit, often denoted by $\gamma$, is called Euler's constant. Its numerical value is $0.5772...$. It is not known whether $\gamma$ is rational or not.)

Since the minimum value of $\dfrac{1}{x}$ on the interval $[n, n+1]$ is $\dfrac{1}{n+1}$, we have for $n=1,2, \cdots$

\begin{align} \frac{1}{n+1} \times (n+1 - n) &\leq \int_{n}^{n+1} \frac{1}{x} dx \\ \frac{1}{n+1} &\leq \sbracket{\ln x}_{n}^{n+1} \\ \frac{1}{n+1} &\leq \ln (n+1) - \ln n\\ \end{align}

Hence we get

\begin{align} \frac{1}{2} &\leq \log 2 - \ln 1 = \ln 2\\ \frac{1}{3} &\leq \ln 3 - \ln 2\\ \frac{1}{4} &\leq \ln 4 - \ln 3\\ \frac{1}{5} &\leq \ln 5 - \ln 4\\ \cdots\\ \frac{1}{N-1} &\leq \ln (N-1) - \ln (N-2)\\ \frac{1}{N} &\leq \ln N - \ln (N-1) \end{align}

Adding them gives

\begin{align} \frac{1}{2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{N} &\leq \ln N \\ 1 +\frac{1}{2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{N} &\leq 1 + \ln N \\ s_N &\leq 1 + \ln N\\ s_N - \ln N &\leq 1 \end{align}
