6(a). Suppose $f(x)f(y) = f(x+y)$ for all real $x$ and $y$. Assuming that $f$ is differentiable and not zero, prove that $f(x)=e^{cx}$, where $c$ is a constant.
$$f(x) = f(x+0) = f(x) f(0)$$ Since $f$ is not zero, we get $1 = f(0)$. $$\begin{align} f'(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x)f(h) -f(x)}{h}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x)(f(h) - 1)}{h}\\ &= f(x) \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(h) - 1}{h}\\ &= f(x) \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(h + 0) - f(0)}{h}\\ &= f(x) f'(0) \\ \end{align}$$

Let $c = f'(0)$. Then $f'(x) = cf(x)$

Let $g(x)=e^{cx}$ for real $x$. Then $g'(x) = c e^{cx} =c g(x)$

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} \dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)} &= \frac{g(x) f'(x) - f(x) g'(x)}{g^2(x)} \\ &= \frac{g(x)cf(x) - f(x)c g(x)}{g^2(x)} \\ &= 0 \\ \dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)} &= K, \text{ where $K$ is arbitrary constant.} \end{align}$$

Since $f(0)= 1$ and $g(0) = e^0 = 1$, $K = \frac{f(0)}{g(0)} = 1$

Hence $f(x) = g(x) = e^{cx}$, where $c$ is a constant.
