Suppose $f$ is a real continuous function on $R^1, f_n(t)=f(nt)$ for $n=1,2,3,...$, and $\{f_n\}$ is equicontinuous on $[0.1]$. What conclusion can you draw about $f$?

We can conclude that the function $f$ must be constant on $[0, \infty)$

Let $\ep > 0$.

Since $\{f_n\}$ is equicontinuous on $[0,1]$, there exists $\delta >0$ such that $$ n \in \N, 0 \leq, z,w \leq 1 , \mod{z-w} < \delta \text{ implies } \mod{f_n(z) - f_n(w)} < \ep $$

For all $x,y \in [0, \infty)$, if $N$ is large enough, $\dfrac{x}{N}, \dfrac{y}{N} \in [0,1]$ and $\mod{\dfrac{x}{N}-\dfrac{y}{N}} < \delta$. Hence we have

$$ \begin{align*} 0 \leq, \dfrac{x}{N},\dfrac{y}{N} \leq 1 , \mod{\dfrac{x}{N}-\dfrac{y}{N}} < \delta \text{ implies } \mod{f_N(\dfrac{x}{N})-f_N(\dfrac{y}{N})} &< \ep \\ \mod{f(N\dfrac{x}{N})-f(N\dfrac{y}{N})} &< \ep \\ \mod{f(x)-f(y)} &< \ep \\ \end{align*} $$

Since $\ep$ is arbitrary, $f(x)=f(y)$.
