--- title: "Theorem 7.26: THE STONE-WEIERSTRASS THEOREM" order: 26 parent: theStoneWeierstrassTheorem ---
If $f$ is a continuous complex function on $[a,b]$, there exists a sequence of polynomials $P_n$ such that $\limit P_n(x) = f(x)$ uniformly on $[a,b]$. If $f$ is real, th $P_n$ may be taken real.

Without loss of generality, we may assume that $[a,b]=[0,1]$. We may also assume that $f(0) = f(1)=0$. For if the theorem is proved for this case, consider $$g(x)=f(x)-f(0)-x(f(1) - f(0))$$ for $0 \in [0,1]$. Here $g(0)=g(1)=0$ and $g$ can be obtained as the limit of a uniformly convergent sequece of polynomials, the same is true of $f$ since $f-g$ is a polynomial.

Furthermore, we define $f(x)$ to be zero for $x \notin [0,1]$. Then $f$ is uniformly continuous on the whole line.

Put $\fQ_n(x) = c_n(1-x^2)^n$ for $n=1,2,3,\dots$, where $c_n$ is chosen so that $\int_{-1}^{1}\fQ_n(x)dx =1$ for $n=1,2,3, \dots$

Set $P_n(x)=\int_{-1}^{1}f(x+t) \mathcal{Q}_n(t) dt$, ($0 \leq \mod{x} \leq 1$)

If $0 < x+t < 1$, then $-x < t < 1-x$.

$$\begin{align*} P_n(x) &= \int_{-1}^{1}f(x+t) \mathcal{Q}_n(t) dt\\ &= \int_{-1}^{-x} f(x+t) \mathcal{Q}_n(t) dt + \int_{-x}^{1-x}f(x+t) \mathcal{Q}_n(t) dt + \int_{1-x}^{1}f(x+t) \mathcal{Q}_n(t) dt\\ &= \int_{-x}^{1-x}f(x+t) \mathcal{Q}_n(t) dt \mbox{ (because $f(x)$ is zero for $x$ outside $[0,1]$)}\\ \end{align*}$$ $$\begin{align*} \mbox{Let } u &= x+t \\ du &= dt \\ t&= -x & &\implies& u &= 0\\ t&= 1-x & &\implies& u &= 1 \end{align*}$$ $$\begin{align*} P_n(x) &=\int_{-x}^{1-x} f(x+t) \mathcal{Q}_n(t) dt\\ &= \int_0^1 f(u) \mathcal{Q}_n(u-x) du\\ &= \int_{0}^{1} f(t) \mathcal{Q}_n(t-x) dt \\ &= \int_{0}^{1} f(t) c_n(1-(t-x)^2)^n dt \\ \end{align*}$$

Hence, clearly, $P_n(x)$ is polynomial in $x$. $\{P_n \}$ is a sequence of polynomials which are real if $f$ is real.

Let $h(x) = (1-x^2)^n-1+nx^2$. Then $h(0)=0$ and $h'(x) = n(1-x^2)^{n-1}(-2x)+2nx =2nx(1-(1-x^2)^{n-1})$.

\text{If } \ 0 &<& x &<& 1 \\ 0 &<& x^2 &<& 1 \\ 0 &>& -x^2 &>& -1 \\ 1 &>& 1-x^2 &>& 0 \\ 1 &>& (1-x^2)^{n-1} &>& 0 \\ -1 &<& -(1-x^2)^{n-1} &<& 0 \\ 0 &<& 1-(1-x^2)^{n-1} &<& 1 \\ 0 &<& 2nx(1-(1-x^2)^{n-1}) &<& 2nx
Since $h(0)=0$ and $h'(x)$ is positive on $(0,1)$, we get $h(x) \geq 0$ for $x \in [0,1]$. Therefore $(1-x^2)^n-1+nx^2 \geq 0$ and hence $(1-x^2)^n \geq 1-nx^2$.

$$\begin{align*} \int_{-1}^{1} (1-x^2)^n dx &= 2 \int_{0}^{1} (1-x^2)^n dx \\ &\geq 2 \int_{0}^{\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}} (1-x^2)^n dx \\ &\geq 2 \int_{0}^{\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}} (1-nx^2) dx\\ &= 2 \left[x-\frac{nx^3}{3}\right]_{0}^{\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}} \\ &= 2\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}-\frac{1}{3\sqrt{n}} \right) \\ &= \frac{4}{3\sqrt{n}} > \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \\ \int_{-1}^{1} (1-x^2)^n dx &> \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \\ \int_{-1}^{1} c_n (1-x^2)^n dx &> \frac{c_n}{\sqrt{n}} \\ \int_{-1}^{1} \fQ_n(x) dx &> \frac{c_n}{\sqrt{n}} \\ 1&> \frac{c_n}{\sqrt{n}}\\ c_n &< \sqrt{n} \tag{1} \end{align*}$$

\text{If } \ \delta &\leq& \mod{x} &\leq &1\\ \delta^2 &\leq& x^2 & \leq& 1\\ -\delta^2 &\geq& -x^2 & \geq& -1\\ 1-\delta^2 &\geq& 1-x^2 & \geq& 0\\ (1-\delta^2)^n &\geq& (1-x^2)^n & \geq& 0\\
$$\mbox{Hence } (1-x^2)^n \leq (1-\delta^2)^n \tag{2}$$ $$\therefore \mbox{From (1) and (2)} \fQ_n(x) = c_n(1-x^2)^n < \sqrt{n}(1-\delta^2)^n \text{ for } \delta \leq \mod{x} \leq 1 \tag{3}$$ $$\therefore \sqrt{n}(1-\delta^2)^n = \sup_{\delta \leq \mod{x} \leq 1} \abs{\fQ_n(x)}$$ $$\begin{align*} \mbox{For } n=1,2,3,\dots,\\ \sqrt{n} &\leq n\\ \sqrt{n}(1-\delta^2)^n &\leq n(1-\delta^2)^n \\ \lim_{n \to \infty} n(1-\delta^2)^n &= \limit \frac{n}{(\frac{1}{1-\delta^2})^n} \bracket{\mbox{indeterminate form }\frac{\infty}{\infty}}\\ &= \limit \frac{1}{\ln(\frac{1}{1-\delta^2}) (\frac{1}{1-\delta^2})^n}\\ &= \frac{1}{\ln(\frac{1}{1-\delta^2})} \limit ({1-\delta^2})^n\\ &= 0 \mbox{ (because $1-\delta^2 < 1$)}\\ \end{align*}$$

Since $\sqrt{n}(1-\delta^2)^n \leq n(1-\delta^2)^n$ and $n(1-\delta^2)^n \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$, $\sqrt{n}(1-\delta^2)^n \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$.

Since $\sqrt{n}(1-\delta^2)^n = \sup\limits_{\delta \leq \mod{x} \leq 1} \abs{\fQ_n(x)-0}$ and $\sqrt{n}(1-\delta^2)^n \to 0$ as $n\to \infty$, by 7.9, $\fQ_n \to 0$ uniformly in $\delta \leq \mod{x} \leq 1$.

Since $f$ is uniformly continuous for every $\varepsilon >0$, there exist a $\delta > 0$ such that

$\mod{y-x} < \delta$ implies that $\mod{f(y)-f(x)}<\frac{\varepsilon}{2}$

Hence if $-\delta < t < \delta$, then $\abs{t} = \abs{(x+t)-x} < \delta$ and hence $\mod{f(x+t)-f(x)}<\frac{\varepsilon}{2}$

Let $M= \sup \abs{f(x)}$. Then $\abs{f(x)} \leq M$ for all $x$.

$$\begin{align*} \mbox{For } 0 \leq x \leq 1, \\ \mod{P_n(x)-f(x)} &= \mod{\int_{-1}^{1} f(x+t)\fQ_n(t)dt -f(x) \int_{-1}^{1}\fQ_n(t)dt}\\ &= \mod{\int_{-1}^{1} (f(x+t)-f(x))\fQ_n(t)dt}\\ &\leq \int_{-1}^{1} \mod{f(x+t)-f(x)} \fQ_n(t) dt\\ &= \int_{-1}^{-\delta} \mod{f(x+t)-f(x)} \fQ_n(t) dt + \int_{-\delta}^{\delta} \mod{f(x+t)-f(x)} \fQ_n(t) dt \\ &+ \int_{\delta}^{1} \mod{f(x+t)-f(x)} \fQ_n(t) dt \\ &\leq \int_{-1}^{-\delta} (\mod{f(x+t)}+\abs{f(x)}) \fQ_n(t) dt + \int_{-\delta}^{\delta} \frac{\ep}{2} \fQ_n(t) dt \\ &+ \int_{\delta}^{1} (\mod{f(x+t)}+\abs{f(x)}) \fQ_n(t) dt \\ &\leq \int_{-1}^{-\delta} (M+M) \fQ_n(t) dt + \frac{\ep}{2} \int_{-\delta}^{\delta} \fQ_n(t) dt + \int_{\delta}^{1} (M+M) \fQ_n(t) dt \\ &\leq 2M \int_{-1}^{-\delta} \fQ_n(t) dt + \frac{\ep}{2} \int_{-1}^{1} \fQ_n(t) dt + 2M \int_{\delta}^{1} \fQ_n(t) dt \\ &= 2M \int_{-1}^{-\delta} \fQ_n(t) dt + \frac{\ep}{2} + 2M \int_{\delta}^{1} \fQ_n(t) dt \\ &< 2M \int_{-1}^{-\delta} \sqrt{n}(1-\delta^2)^n dt + \frac{\ep}{2} + 2M \int_{\delta}^{1} \sqrt{n}(1-\delta^2)^n dt \mbox{ (because from (3))} \\ &= 2M \sqrt{n}(1-\delta^2)^n (-\delta + 1) + \frac{\ep}{2} + 2M \sqrt{n}(1-\delta^2)^n (1- \delta) \\ &= 4M \sqrt{n} (1-\delta^2)^n (-\delta + 1) + \frac{\ep}{2} \\ &< \ep \mbox{ for all large enough }n \end{align*}$$
