--- title: "7.6: Is the integral of limit function equal to the limit function of the integrals?" order: 5 parent: discussionOfMainProblem ---
Let $f_n:[0,1] \to \R$ be defined by $f_n(x)=n^2x(1-x^2)^n, n=1,2,3,\dots$. Are $\limit \int\limits_{0}^{1}f_n(x) dx$ and $\int_{0}^{1} \limit f_n(x) dx$ equal?

Let $f_n:[0,1] \to \R$ be defined by $f_n(x)=n^2x(1-x^2)^n, n=1,2,3,\dots$. $$\begin{align*} \int_{0}^{1} f_n(x) dx &= \int_{0}^{1} n^2x(1-x^2)^n dx \\ \text{Let } u &= 1-x^2 \\ du &= -2x dx\\ -\frac{1}{2} du &= xdx\\ \text{If } x=0, u&= 1-0^2 =1\\ \text{If } x=1, u&= 1-1^2 =0\\ \int_{0}^{1} f_n(x) dx &= \int_{1}^{0}n^2u^n\frac{-1}{2}du \\ &=\frac{-n^2}{2} \sbracket{ \frac{u^{n+1}}{n+1}}_{u=1}^{u=0}\\ &= \frac{-n^2}{2} \bracket{0-\frac{1}{n+1}}\\ &= \frac{n^2}{2n+2}\\ \limit \int_0^1 f_n(x) dx &= \limit \frac{n^2}{2n+2} =\limit \frac{1}{\frac{2}{n}+\frac{2}{n^2}} \to \infty \end{align*}$$

$$\begin{align*} \text{If }x=0,\ &f_n(0)=0 \text{ for all } n\\ \text{If }x=1,\ &f_n(1)=0 \text{ for all } n\\ \text{If }0 < x < 1,\ & 0 < x^2 < 1\\ &0 > -x^2 > -1 \\ &1 > 1-x^2 > 0\\ &0 < 1-x^2 <1 \\ & \frac{1}{1-x^2} > 1 \\ \limit n^2x(1-x^2)^n &= x\limit \frac{n^2}{(\frac{1}{1-x^2})^n}\\ &\bracket{\text{indeterminate form } \frac{\infty}{\infty}}\\ &= x\limit \frac{2n}{log(\frac{1}{1-x^2})(\frac{1}{1-x^2})^n}\\ &\bracket{\text{indeterminate form } \frac{\infty}{\infty}}\\ &= x \limit \frac{2}{\bracket{log(\frac{1}{1-x^2})}^2(\frac{1}{1-x^2})^n}\\ &= \frac{2x}{\bracket{log(\frac{1}{1-x^2})}^2}\limit(1-x^2)^n \\ &= 0 \ (\because 1-x^2 < 1)\\ \therefore \limit f_n(x) &= 0 \forall x \in [0,1]\\ \int_0^{1} \limit f_n(x) dx &=0 \end{align*}$$

So $\limit \int\limits_{0}^{1}f_n(x) dx \neq \int_{0}^{1} \limit f_n(x) dx$
